"The surface of a Vermeer such as the National Gallery's
Woman Holding a Balance still looks “perfect and inevitable,”
Wieseman explains. But microscopic examination of the museum's
Vermeers conducted since the 1990s have shown that Vermeer
worked in discrete stages. The most recent analyses offer
more complete images of these stages in the painting process.
Beneath the finished exterior, scientists are now able to
provide images of the preparatory sketch (a monochrome
painted sketch) and the underpaint.

perfect. discrete. monochrome.

Here is the revelation that left the team wide-eyed.
Under the surface, they can see evidence of quick,
sketchy, spontaneous, and sometimes thickly textured
brushstrokes. It's as if Vermeer shifted elements
around in an impetuous process of discovery, trying
and rejecting different approaches—the opposite of
the painstakingly slow perfectionist who proceeded
molecule by molecule to achieve the timeless beauty
of his finished surface. So, what makes a Vermeer
a Vermeer? An initial energetic, inspired, creative
phase, it seems. For Vermeer, arriving at that
signature effervescent depiction of light turns
out to have been a complex, multiphase journey,
creating and revising layers that serve as a
foundation for the finished work we see today.
Thanks to the team's latest advanced scientific
analysis, it may be time to reevaluate Vermeer's
technique. And perhaps Vermeer himself.
